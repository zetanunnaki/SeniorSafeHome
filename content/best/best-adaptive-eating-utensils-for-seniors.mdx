  - question: "What utensils are best for hand tremors or Parkinson's?"
    answer: "Weighted utensils. The added mass (around 5–7 oz per piece) stabilizes a shaky hand, dramatically reducing spills. They look like ordinary silverware, so they work well when dining out. For a very weak grip, look for a set that includes universal cuffs that strap the utensil to the hand."
  - question: "What helps a senior with arthritis eat more easily?"
    answer: "Built-up foam-handle utensils need far less grip strength than thin metal handles, and a curved rocker knife cuts food with a rocking motion instead of sawing. Pair them with an easy-grip jar opener for the rest of the kitchen."
  - question: "What is a scoop plate?"
    answer: "A scoop plate has one high, curved wall so food can be pushed against it and scooped onto the utensil in a single motion — ideal for one-handed eating after a stroke, or for anyone with tremors. A non-slip base keeps the plate from sliding."
  - question: "Why high-contrast plates?"
    answer: "Bold, high-contrast colors help a senior with low vision or dementia see where the food is on the plate, which can improve appetite and independence. White food on a dark plate (or vice versa) is much easier to see."

## Match the aid to the need

- **Tremors / Parkinson's** — weighted utensils whose mass steadies the hand.
- **Arthritis / weak grip** — built-up foam-handle utensils and a rocker knife.
- **One-handed / stroke recovery** — a scoop plate with a high wall to gather food.
- **Low vision / dementia** — high-contrast plates that make food easy to see.
- **Stiff hands in the kitchen** — an easy-grip jar opener.
