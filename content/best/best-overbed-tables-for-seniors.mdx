An overbed table turns a bed into a comfortable, independent space — for meals, reading, a laptop, or keeping water and medications within reach during recovery. Because it reduces how often a senior needs to get up, the right table also lowers fall risk. We compared hospital-trusted rolling tables, cross-bed models for large beds, and tilt-tops on stability, height range, and capacity.

<Callout type="tip" title="Check your bed size first">
Standard rolling tables roll under the side of most twin–queen beds. For **queen and king** beds, you'll need an adjustable-length cross-bed table whose legs straddle the mattress.
</Callout>

## Which type fits your need?

- **Classic rolling table** — recovery, eating in bed, standard twin–queen beds; the hospital-familiar choice.
- **Adjustable-length (cross-bed)** — essential for queen and king beds; legs straddle the mattress.
- **Tilt-top** — reading, tablets, and writing in bed; the angle eases neck strain.
- **High-capacity (350 lb)** — heavier laptops, trays, or medical equipment.

## How to choose

1. **Start with bed size** — standard rolling table for twin–queen; cross-bed table for queen/king.
2. **Check the height range** — make sure it clears your mattress with room to spare.
3. **Match the capacity** — 350 lb models for heavier laptops, trays, or equipment.
4. **Pick the top** — a tilt-top helps for reading; a flat top is steadier for meals.
