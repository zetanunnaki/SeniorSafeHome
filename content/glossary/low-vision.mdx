**Low vision** describes a level of visual impairment that cannot be fully corrected with ordinary glasses, contact lenses, medication, or surgery, yet still leaves usable sight. It is not the same as blindness — people with low vision retain some vision that the right aids can make much more functional.

## Common causes

In older adults, low vision often results from [macular degeneration](/glossary/macular-degeneration), glaucoma, diabetic retinopathy, or cataracts. The effect varies: some people lose central vision, others lose peripheral vision or contrast sensitivity.

## Living well with low vision

Two principles help most: **magnify** what's too small, and add **light and contrast** to what's too faint. Illuminated and electronic [magnifiers](/best/best-low-vision-magnifiers-for-seniors), bright task lighting, and large high-contrast devices restore many everyday tasks. A low-vision specialist or occupational therapist can recommend aids tailored to a person's remaining sight. See our [low-vision aids guide](/guides/low-vision-aids-for-seniors).
