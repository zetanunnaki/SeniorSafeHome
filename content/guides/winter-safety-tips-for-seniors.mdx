Winter brings real, often underestimated risks for older adults: icy falls, hypothermia that can set in even indoors, heating hazards, and the isolation of being stuck inside. The good news is that a handful of sensible precautions make the cold months far safer. This guide covers what seniors and caregivers should do to stay warm, steady, and well all winter.

## Why winter is riskier as we age

Aging changes how the body handles cold. Older adults sense temperature less accurately, regulate body heat less efficiently, and have thinner skin and less insulating fat. Many also take medications or live with conditions — thyroid disease, diabetes, heart or circulation problems — that further blunt temperature control. The result: a senior can become dangerously cold at temperatures a younger person would barely notice, sometimes inside their own home. Add slippery surfaces and shorter, darker days, and winter calls for extra care.

## Preventing falls on ice and snow

Falls are the leading cause of injury for older adults, and ice multiplies the danger.

- **Stay in during and just after storms.** The safest approach is to avoid icy surfaces entirely. Arrange for grocery delivery, prescription delivery, or a helper for errands during bad weather.
- **Wear the right footwear.** Boots or shoes with deep, non-slip tread make a real difference; avoid smooth soles. Indoors, [non-slip grip socks](/best/best-non-slip-grip-socks-for-seniors) help on cold hard floors.
- **Use a mobility aid with winter traction.** A [cane](/best/best-canes-for-seniors) or [walker](/best/best-walkers-rollators-for-seniors) with an ice-grip tip adds a stable point of contact on slick ground.
- **Keep walkways clear.** Salt or sand steps and paths, or arrange for someone to do it. Add a sturdy [handrail](/best/best-stair-safety-for-seniors) at outdoor steps.
- **Walk like a penguin.** On unavoidable ice, take short, flat-footed steps with your weight slightly forward, and keep your hands free (not in pockets) for balance.
- **Light the way.** With darkness arriving early, good outdoor and entryway lighting helps you see hazards. (See our [fall prevention guide](/guides/fall-prevention-guide-seniors-home).)

## Staying warm and avoiding hypothermia

Hypothermia — a dangerous drop in body temperature — can develop slowly in seniors, even indoors.

- **Keep the home at 68–70°F (20–21°C).** Don't let a senior turn the heat down too far to save money. If cost is a concern, look into heating assistance such as **LIHEAP** in the U.S.
- **Dress in layers,** even inside: several thin layers trap heat better than one thick one. A hat indoors helps too — a lot of heat is lost from the head. Warm [fuzzy grip socks](/best/best-non-slip-grip-socks-for-seniors) keep feet warm and safe on cold floors.
- **Watch for the signs of hypothermia:** cold or pale skin, shivering that stops, slurred speech, confusion, drowsiness, and clumsiness. If you suspect it, warm the person gradually and get medical help.
- **Eat and drink warm.** Regular meals and warm drinks help the body produce heat. (Dehydration is still a risk in winter — see our [nutrition and hydration guide](/guides/nutrition-and-hydration-for-seniors).)

## Heating the home safely

Heating equipment is a leading cause of winter house fires.

- **Space heaters:** keep at least three feet from anything flammable, place on a hard flat surface, never leave running unattended or overnight, and choose models with automatic tip-over and overheat shutoff.
- **Never use an oven or stovetop to heat the home** — a serious carbon-monoxide risk.
- **Test smoke and carbon-monoxide alarms** at the start of winter and replace batteries. CO is odorless and especially dangerous with furnaces, fireplaces, and generators.
- **Have a power-outage plan:** warm blankets, a charged phone, a flashlight, and a way to call for help. Know where to go (a relative's home or a warming center) if heat fails for an extended time.

## Beating winter isolation

Shorter days and bad weather keep many seniors housebound, and isolation harms both mental and physical health.

- **Schedule regular check-ins** — a daily call, a video chat, or a neighbor who stops by.
- **Use technology to stay connected** — a [simple smart display or voice assistant](/best/best-smart-home-devices-for-seniors) makes video calls with family easy.
- **Keep a medical alert device handy.** A [medical alert system](/best/best-medical-alert-systems-for-seniors) is especially valuable in winter, when a fall outdoors or a heating emergency could otherwise go unnoticed.
- **Plan indoor activity.** Gentle movement, hobbies, and light from a [bright daylight lamp](/best/best-daylight-reading-lamps-for-seniors) help counter the winter blues. (More in our guide on [preventing loneliness and isolation](/guides/preventing-loneliness-and-isolation-in-seniors).)

<Callout type="safety" title="Build a simple winter kit">
Put together a small cold-weather kit: a warm blanket, a flashlight with spare batteries, a charged power bank, non-perishable snacks, water, any critical medications, and emergency contact numbers — kept somewhere easy to reach. It turns a power outage or storm from a crisis into an inconvenience.
</Callout>

## A quick winter checklist

1. Footwear with tread; mobility aid with an ice tip; walkways salted.
2. Home at 68–70°F; layers and warm socks; hypothermia signs known.
3. Space heaters used safely; smoke and CO alarms tested.
4. Errands delivered during storms; outdoor lighting working.
5. Daily check-ins set up; medical alert device worn; winter kit ready.

## The bottom line

Winter's risks for seniors — falls, hypothermia, fire, and isolation — are real but very manageable. Warm the home properly, get traction underfoot, heat safely, and stay connected, and the cold months become just another season to enjoy from a safe, cozy home.
