Choosing a mobility aid isn't about the fanciest device — it's about matching the *amount of support* a senior needs to the *places they go*. Too little support risks a fall; too much can weaken muscles and reduce independence. This guide walks through the decision and how to get the fit right.

## The short answer

Match support to need: **a cane for mild balance help, a walker or rollator for real instability, and a transport wheelchair for distances that are unsafe or exhausting.** Many seniors use more than one — a rollator at home and a wheelchair for outings.

## The decision, step by step

1. **Mild unsteadiness or one weaker leg → a [cane](/best/best-canes-for-seniors).** One extra point of contact. Quad or pivoting-base canes add stability and can stand on their own.
2. **More instability, or needing to take weight off the legs → a [walker or rollator](/best/best-walkers-rollators-for-seniors).** A standard walker is most stable indoors; a rollator rolls smoothly, has brakes and a seat, and suits longer distances and outdoors.
3. **Walking distances is unsafe or exhausting → a [transport wheelchair](/best/best-transport-wheelchairs-for-seniors).** Caregiver-pushed, lightweight, and folds for the car.

<Callout type="tip" title="It's not all-or-nothing">
Needs change by setting and by day. It's common — and smart — to use a cane or rollator at home and keep a transport wheelchair for appointments, the mall, or travel. Pick for the hardest trip, not the easiest.
</Callout>

## Canes

Best for mild balance issues or offloading one sore hip or knee.

- **Standard** canes are light and simple.
- **Quad and pivoting-base** canes stand on their own and add stability.
- **Folding** canes travel easily and tuck into a bag.

Get the [right cane](/best/best-canes-for-seniors), set the height to the wrist crease, and make sure the tip is a fresh, non-slip rubber.

## Walkers and rollators

Best for real instability or when a senior needs to lean for support.

- **Standard walkers** (no wheels or two front wheels) are the most stable — ideal indoors and for those who need to bear significant weight.
- **Rollators** (four wheels, brakes, seat) roll smoothly and let the user rest, but require enough hand strength to work the brakes. Best for longer distances and outdoors.

## Transport wheelchairs

Best when walking any distance is unsafe or exhausting.

- **Lightweight aluminum** models (15–22 lb) lift easily into a car.
- **Caregiver-pushed** with small rear wheels — lighter and more compact than a self-propelled wheelchair.

## Getting the fit right

Fit matters as much as the device:

- **Height:** the grip should reach the wrist crease, leaving a slight elbow bend (~15–20°).
- **Tips and wheels:** replace worn cane tips; keep rollator wheels and brakes in good order.
- **Weight capacity:** confirm the rating comfortably exceeds the user's weight; bariatric options exist.
- **The home:** clear clutter and loose rugs so any aid can move safely.

<Callout type="safety" title="Ask a physical therapist">
A physical therapist can assess gait and balance, recommend the right aid, and set it up correctly — sometimes covered by insurance with a doctor's referral. The wrong aid or wrong height can actually increase fall risk, so a quick professional check is worth it.
</Callout>
