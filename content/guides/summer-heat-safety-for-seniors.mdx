Hot weather is a serious, underappreciated danger for older adults — heat waves cause more deaths than many other natural hazards, and seniors are the most affected. The reason is biological: aging blunts the body's ability to cool itself and to sense heat and thirst. The good news is that heat illness is highly preventable with a few deliberate habits. This guide covers how to keep a senior safe, cool, and hydrated through summer.

## Why heat hits seniors harder

As we age, several defenses weaken at once:

- **We sweat less,** so the body's main cooling system is less effective.
- **The sense of heat and thirst fades,** so a senior may be dangerously overheated without feeling hot or thirsty.
- **Chronic conditions** (heart, lung, kidney) and common **medications** (diuretics, beta-blockers, some antidepressants and antihistamines) further impair temperature regulation.

That combination means an older adult can develop heat exhaustion or heat stroke at temperatures a younger person tolerates easily — sometimes inside a poorly cooled home.

## Know the warning signs

### Heat exhaustion (serious — act now)
- Heavy sweating, cool or clammy skin
- Weakness, fatigue, dizziness
- Headache, nausea, muscle cramps
- Fast, weak pulse

**What to do:** Move to a cool place, lie down, loosen clothing, sip water, and apply cool wet cloths. If symptoms worsen or don't improve within an hour, seek medical care.

### Heat stroke (medical emergency — call 911)
- Body temperature of 103°F (39.4°C) or higher
- Hot, red skin that may be dry (sweating can stop)
- Confusion, slurred speech, agitation
- Rapid strong pulse; possible fainting or seizures

**What to do:** **Call 911 immediately.** While waiting, move the person to shade or indoors, remove excess clothing, and cool them rapidly with cool water, wet cloths, or ice packs at the neck, armpits, and groin. Do not give fluids if they're confused or unconscious.

## How to stay cool

- **Use air conditioning during heat waves.** If the home isn't air-conditioned, spend the hottest hours (roughly 10 a.m.–6 p.m.) somewhere that is — a public cooling center, library, senior center, or mall. Even a few hours in air conditioning helps the body recover.
- **Block the heat.** Close blinds and curtains during the day, open up in the cooler evening, and use fans to move air. Note: in very high heat (above the mid-90s °F), a fan alone may not prevent heat illness — pair it with other cooling.
- **Cool the body directly.** Cool (not cold) showers, damp cloths on the neck and wrists, a spray bottle of water, and [cooling gel seat cushions](/best/best-seat-cushions-for-seniors) all lower body temperature.
- **Dress for heat.** Lightweight, loose, light-colored, breathable clothing; a wide-brimmed hat and sunscreen outdoors.

## Stay hydrated

- **Drink on a schedule, not by thirst** — a glass with each meal and in between, since thirst is unreliable in seniors.
- **Favor water;** water-rich foods (melon, cucumber, soup) count too.
- **Go easy on alcohol and large amounts of caffeine,** which worsen dehydration.
- **Keep fluids within reach** — a marked water bottle, or a [cup holder on a walker or wheelchair](/best/best-walker-accessories-for-seniors), makes sipping effortless. Our [nutrition and hydration guide](/guides/nutrition-and-hydration-for-seniors) has more.

<Callout type="safety" title="Watch out for medications and the heat">
Some common medications make heat more dangerous — diuretics increase fluid loss, and others affect sweating or blood pressure. Don't stop any medication on your own, but do ask the doctor or pharmacist whether a senior's prescriptions raise their heat risk, and what to do during a heat wave.
</Callout>

## Plan ahead for heat waves

- **Check in daily** during extreme heat — a call or visit. Many heat deaths happen to seniors living alone whose decline went unnoticed.
- **Know the forecast** and have a cooling plan before the heat arrives, including where to go if the home gets too hot.
- **Keep a [medical alert device](/best/best-medical-alert-systems-for-seniors) handy** so help is one button away if a senior feels unwell.
- **Never leave anyone — or a pet — in a parked car,** even briefly; interior temperatures rise to deadly levels within minutes.
- **Watch high-risk days:** the first hot days of summer (before the body adapts) and multi-day heat waves are the most dangerous.

## A quick summer checklist

1. Cooling plan set; air conditioning or a cooling center identified.
2. Hydration on a schedule; fluids within reach.
3. Heat-illness signs known; 911 plan for heat stroke.
4. Blinds closed by day; lightweight clothing; cool showers/cloths.
5. Daily check-ins during heat waves; medications reviewed for heat risk.

## The bottom line

Heat is one of the most dangerous — and most preventable — summer risks for seniors. Because older bodies cool and signal thirst poorly, don't rely on how a senior *feels*. Build cooling and hydration into the daily routine, know the emergency signs, and check in often during heat waves. A little planning keeps summer safe and enjoyable.
