Falls are the leading cause of injury for older adults — but the large majority happen at home, where they're also very preventable. This guide walks through the whole house, room by room, with the changes that reduce the most risk. Start with the bathroom; it's where falls are most common and most serious.

## The short answer

Three changes prevent the most falls: **make footing non-slip (especially in the bathroom), light every path the senior walks, and add sturdy support handles where they stand, transfer, and bathe.** Everything below builds on those three principles.

## Bathroom (highest priority)

The bathroom causes more senior falls than any other room. Address it first:

1. **Add a [shower chair or bench](/best/best-shower-chairs-for-seniors)** so bathing happens sitting down.
2. **Install [grab bars](/best/best-grab-bars-for-seniors)** at the shower entry and beside the toilet — weight-bearing, anchored into studs.
3. **Put down a [non-slip bath mat](/best/best-non-slip-bath-mats-for-seniors)** and a non-slip rug outside the tub.
4. **Add a [raised toilet seat](/best/best-raised-toilet-seats-for-seniors)** if standing from the toilet is a strain.
5. **Lower the water heater to 120°F** to prevent scalds.

See our full [bathroom fall-prevention guide](/guides/prevent-bathroom-falls-seniors) for details.

## Bedroom

1. **Set the bed to a safe height** — feet flat, knees near 90° when sitting on the edge.
2. **Add a [bed rail](/best/best-bed-rails-for-seniors)** for getting in and out.
3. **Light the path to the bathroom** with motion-activated night lights.
4. **Keep a lamp, phone, and glasses within reach.** Full details in our [bedroom safety checklist](/guides/bedroom-safety-checklist-seniors).

## Hallways, stairs, and living areas

1. **Remove trip hazards** — loose throw rugs, cords, and clutter.
2. **Light every hallway and stairway,** with switches at both ends and motion night lights.
3. **Add handrails on both sides of stairs,** and mark step edges with high-contrast tape.
4. **Arrange furniture** so there's a clear, wide walking path.
5. **Keep daily items at waist height** so there's no climbing or deep bending — a [reacher](/best/best-reachers-for-seniors) helps retrieve anything low or high.

## Kitchen

1. **Store everyday items between knee and shoulder height** — no step stools.
2. **Wipe spills immediately** and use non-slip mats at the sink.
3. **Keep a [reacher](/best/best-reachers-for-seniors) handy** for higher shelves.

## Footwear, vision, and health

- **Supportive, non-slip shoes** indoors — never socks alone on hard floors.
- **Annual eye checks** and up-to-date glasses; poor vision is a major fall factor.
- **Medication review** — some drugs cause dizziness or drowsiness. Ask the pharmacist.
- **Strength and balance exercise** (with a doctor's okay) measurably reduces falls.

## Have a plan if a fall happens

<Callout type="safety" title="Be ready, not just prepared">
Even a well-prepared home can't prevent every fall. A [medical alert device](/best/best-medical-alert-systems-for-seniors) lets a senior summon help if they fall and can't reach a phone — especially important for anyone living alone.
</Callout>

## When to involve a professional

After a fall, a new diagnosis, or a change in mobility, ask the doctor about an occupational therapy assessment. An OT tailors recommendations to the specific home and person — see our glossary entry on the [occupational therapist](/glossary/occupational-therapist).
