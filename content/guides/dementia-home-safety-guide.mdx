Caring for a loved one with dementia or Alzheimer's at home means balancing two things that can feel opposed: **safety and dignity.** The goal isn't to lock down the house — it's to quietly remove the highest-stakes risks so your loved one stays independent for as long as possible, and you can rest. This guide walks through the home room by room.

## The short answer

Three risks matter most in dementia care: **wandering, medication errors, and nighttime falls.** Address them first — a door/wander alarm, a locking pill dispenser, and good nighttime lighting cover the large majority of emergencies, and all three are inexpensive.

## Exits and entryways (highest priority)

Wandering is the highest-stakes dementia risk — a person can leave unnoticed in seconds, especially at night. Layer two simple alarms:

1. **Add a [door or wander alarm](/best/best-door-alarms-for-dementia)** that chimes when an exterior door opens. Choose one with a separate caregiver receiver so you hear it in another room.
2. **Add a motion sensor** near exits that alerts you as your loved one *approaches* — before the door is even touched.
3. **For memory-care settings or higher risk,** use a professional exit stopper with a key-operated override.

<Callout type="tip" title="Identify which door opened">
Some systems (like multi-sensor caregiver pagers) let you assign a different chime to each door, so you instantly know whether it's the front door or the bathroom — valuable in a larger home.
</Callout>

## Kitchen and medications

Medication mistakes — missed doses, double doses, or taking the wrong pills — are common and dangerous in dementia.

1. **Use a [locking automatic pill dispenser](/best/best-automatic-pill-dispensers-for-seniors)** that reveals only the dose that's due and sounds an alarm. A mix-proof, key-locked model is designed specifically for memory care.
2. **Choose an app-connected dispenser** if you live apart, so you're alerted when a dose is missed.
3. **Reduce kitchen hazards** — consider stove knob covers, an auto-shutoff kettle, and removing or securing sharp tools.

## Bedroom and nighttime

Most nighttime incidents start with getting up in the dark and becoming disoriented.

- **Light the path** with [motion-sensor night lights](/best/best-motion-sensor-night-lights-for-seniors) from the bed to the bathroom — warm, glare-free, automatic.
- **Shorten the trip** with a [bedside commode](/best/best-bedside-commodes-for-seniors) to reduce nighttime walking, a leading cause of falls.
- **Add a [bed rail](/best/best-bed-rails-for-seniors)** for safer, steadier transfers in and out of bed.
- **Use a bed or door alarm** so you're alerted if your loved one gets up and starts to wander.

## Bathroom

The bathroom is the most common room for falls for *all* seniors, and confusion adds risk for those with dementia.

- **Add [grab bars](/best/best-grab-bars-for-seniors)** at the shower entry and beside the toilet.
- **Use a [shower chair](/best/best-shower-chairs-for-seniors)** so bathing happens sitting down.
- **Simplify** — reduce clutter, use contrasting colors to make fixtures easy to find, and consider labeling.

See our full [bathroom fall-prevention guide](/guides/prevent-bathroom-falls-seniors) for the details.

## Living areas and general home

- **Clear pathways** of clutter, cords, and loose rugs — the most common trip hazards.
- **Use contrast** — dementia affects depth and color perception; contrasting tape on step edges and brightly colored plates can help.
- **Keep a [medical alert system](/best/best-medical-alert-systems-for-seniors)** within reach, and consider one with fall detection for early-stage independence.

## Build your dementia safety layers

A complete, low-cost setup looks like this:

1. **Exits:** door alarm + motion sensor with a caregiver receiver.
2. **Medications:** locking automatic pill dispenser (app-connected if you're remote).
3. **Nighttime:** motion-sensor night lights + bedside commode + bed/door alarm.
4. **Bathroom:** grab bars + shower chair.

<Callout type="safety" title="Involve the care team">
Every person's dementia is different. A doctor or occupational therapist can assess the specific home and stage, and recommend the right level of monitoring. If wandering has already occurred, ask about local safe-return programs and GPS options.
</Callout>
