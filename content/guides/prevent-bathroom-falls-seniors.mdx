The bathroom is the most common room for serious falls among older adults — wet, hard surfaces, tight spaces, and tasks that require balance all combine in one small room. The good news is that bathroom falls are also among the most preventable. This guide walks through the changes that reduce the most risk, roughly in order of impact, so you can start with what matters most.

## The short answer

If you do only three things, do these: **add a stable shower chair, install a weight-bearing grab bar at the shower entry, and put down a high-suction non-slip mat.** Together they address the two leading causes of bathroom falls — slippery footing and the strain of standing while bathing.

## Bathroom safety checklist

Work through these in order. Each step builds on the last.

1. **Add a shower chair or bench.** Sitting to bathe removes the need to stand on a wet, slick surface and reduces fatigue. See our [best shower chairs roundup](/best/best-shower-chairs-for-seniors).
2. **Install grab bars where balance is hardest.** Put a weight-bearing bar at the shower or tub entry and another beside the toilet. Read our [best grab bars guide](/best/best-grab-bars-for-seniors).
3. **Put down a non-slip mat.** A high-suction mat inside the tub and a non-slip rug outside it both help. Replace mats when the suction weakens.
4. **Raise the toilet if standing is a strain.** A raised toilet seat reduces how far a senior has to lower and lift their body.
5. **Improve lighting.** Add a bright, glare-free light and a motion-activated night light for safe nighttime trips.
6. **Lower the water heater temperature.** Setting it to 120°F (49°C) reduces scald risk, which is especially important for seniors with reduced sensation.
7. **Clear the clutter.** Remove loose rugs that can slip, and keep frequently used items within easy reach to avoid stretching or bending.

<Callout type="safety" title="Install weight-bearing bars correctly">
A grab bar only protects against a fall if it can hold a person&apos;s full weight. Screw-mounted bars must be anchored into wall studs or solid blocking — never into drywall alone. Suction bars are for light balance only and should not be relied on to catch a fall.
</Callout>

## Address the toilet area too

Standing up from a low toilet strains the hips and knees and is a common moment for a loss of balance. A raised toilet seat with support arms makes the sit-to-stand motion easier and safer, and a grab bar beside the toilet gives a stable handhold.

<Callout type="tip" title="Think about the whole path">
Falls don&apos;t only happen at the tub. Make sure the route from the bedroom to the bathroom is well-lit and clear, especially for nighttime trips. A motion-activated night light along the hallway is inexpensive and effective.
</Callout>

## When to involve a professional

If your loved one has had a fall, a new diagnosis, or a significant change in mobility, consider asking their doctor for a referral to an occupational therapist. An OT can assess the specific home and recommend tailored modifications — and that assessment is sometimes covered by insurance.
