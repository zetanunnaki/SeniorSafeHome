The bedroom is where many senior falls begin — getting in and out of bed, or making a dark, drowsy trip to the bathroom. The good news is that bedroom falls are very preventable. Work through this checklist in order; each step builds on the last.

## The short answer

The three highest-impact changes are: **set the bed to a safe height, add a bed rail for getting in and out, and light the entire path to the bathroom.** Together they address the two riskiest bedroom moments — transferring at the bedside and walking in the dark.

## Bedroom safety checklist

1. **Get the bed height right.** The senior should sit on the edge with both feet flat and knees near 90°. Use bed risers to raise a low bed, or a lower profile to bring a high bed down.
2. **Add a bed rail.** A sturdy [bed assist rail](/best/best-bed-rails-for-seniors) gives something solid to grip when getting in and out, and a full-length rail helps prevent rolling out. Keep it tight against the mattress.
3. **Light the path.** Place motion-activated night lights from the bed to the bathroom so no one walks in the dark or fumbles for a switch.
4. **Clear the route.** Remove loose rugs, cords, and clutter between the bed and door. A trip hazard at 2 a.m. is far more dangerous than in daylight.
5. **Keep essentials within reach.** A lamp, phone, glasses, and water on a stable nightstand mean no stretching or getting up unnecessarily. A [reacher](/best/best-reachers-for-seniors) helps retrieve dropped items without bending.
6. **Consider a bedside commode** if the bathroom is far or nighttime trips are frequent.
7. **Check the floor.** Non-slip flooring or a low-pile, secured rug is safest. Avoid loose throw rugs entirely.

<Callout type="safety" title="Bed rail gap safety">
If you add a bed rail, make sure it sits flush against the mattress with no gap. Gaps between the rail and mattress are the number-one entrapment hazard. Use only ASTM F3186-17-certified rails on home beds.
</Callout>

## When to involve a professional

If your loved one has had a fall, a new diagnosis, or a change in mobility, ask their doctor about an occupational therapy assessment. An OT can recommend the right bed height, transfer technique, and equipment for their specific situation — and that assessment is sometimes covered by insurance.
