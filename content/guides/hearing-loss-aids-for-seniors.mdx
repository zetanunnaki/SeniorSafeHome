Hearing loss creeps up gradually, and many seniors withdraw from conversations long before they admit there's a problem. The good news: a range of affordable devices — from simple amplifiers to captioned phones — can bring back conversations, television, and connection. This guide explains the options and when each makes sense.

## The short answer

Match the tool to the situation: **a personal sound amplifier for conversations and everyday sounds, an amplified/captioned phone for calls, and a TV listening device for television.** For diagnosed or significant hearing loss, see an audiologist about a hearing aid.

## Amplifiers vs. hearing aids

This is the first decision:

- **Personal sound amplifiers (PSAPs)** amplify all nearby sound. They're inexpensive, need no prescription, and are great for conversations, TV, and everyday listening — but they're **not** medical devices.
- **OTC hearing aids** are FDA-cleared and tuned for diagnosed mild-to-moderate hearing loss.
- **Prescription hearing aids** (fitted by an audiologist) are best for significant, one-sided, or complex hearing loss.

<Callout type="safety" title="When to see an audiologist">
If hearing loss is significant, sudden, one-sided, or comes with ringing or dizziness, see a professional before buying an amplifier. A simple amplifier is fine for mild, gradual loss — but a proper evaluation rules out treatable causes and gets the right device.
</Callout>

## Amplifiers for conversation

For talking with family, restaurants, and everyday sounds:

- **Body-worn (pocket)** amplifiers are the easiest to use — a big volume knob, the longest battery life, and a TV cord.
- **Behind-the-ear and in-ear** amplifiers are more discreet and rechargeable, some sold as a pair.

## Phones for hearing loss

The telephone is where hearing loss bites hardest. Two features help most:

- **Amplified volume** — [amplified large-button phones](/best/best-large-button-phones-for-seniors) boost the earpiece far beyond a normal phone.
- **Captioning** — captioned phones show real-time text of what the caller says, so a senior can read along.

## Television and the home

- **TV listening** — many amplifiers include a TV cord, or a dedicated TV listening device sends sound straight to the listener without blasting the room.
- **Doorbells and alerts** — loud or visual (flashing) alerts ensure a senior doesn't miss the door or a phone ring.
- **Quiet the room** — soft furnishings and turning off background noise make speech far easier to follow.

## Putting it together

A practical hearing setup looks like this:

1. **An amplifier** for conversations and everyday sounds (pocket style for the simplest use).
2. **An amplified or captioned phone** for calls.
3. **A TV listening solution** so television is comfortable without disturbing others.
4. **A professional hearing check** if loss is more than mild.
